With NATO warplanes shifted closer for possible NATO airstrikes, U.S. 
envoy Richard Holbrooke described the Kosovo standoff as an ``emergency'' 
on Monday and demanded the Yugoslav leader end his crackdown. After 
a rest of just a few hours, Holbrooke resumed talks with President 
Slobodan Milosevic on defusing the Kosovo crisis and avoiding threatened 
NATO airstrikes on Yugoslav military targets. Holbrooke described 
his earlier overnight meeting with Milosevic as ``intense, indeed, 
and at times very heated.'' He said the standoff between NATO and 
the Yugoslav president ``can only be called an emergency.'' NATO officials 
also met in Brussels, Belgium, first on an informal level in the morning. 
An official conference was scheduled for later in the day, which was 
expected to result in an ``activation order'' for NATO airstrikes 
against Yugoslavia unless diplomacy produces a breakthrough. In Paris, 
French Defense Minister Alain Richard said his country would provide 
around 40 aircraft for possible airstrikes, which could last ``several 
days.'' Foreign presence was being reduced in anticipation of NATO 
action. The last two vehicles of international team of U.S., Russian 
and other diplomats observing events in Kosovo over the past months 
left for neighboring Macedonia before midday, carrying Americans and 
Europeans. Sources at the American embassy said all but 12 people 
would be evacuated from Belgrade to Budapest. The German embassy was 
closed down Monday morning, said local employees, speaking on condition 
of anonymity, while British embassy staff were waiting for the outcome
