While NATO moved to the brink of airstrikes, Slobodan Milosevic reportedly 
softened his stand Saturday on international demands over Kosovo but 
refused to make a key concession in talks with a U.S. envoy. On a 
day of shuttle diplomacy by Richard Holbrooke between Belgrade and 
Pristina, sources close to Milosevic said the Yugoslav president had 
signaled his willingness to comply with six main demands in order 
to head off intervention. Those measures would include withdrawing 
more troops from Kosovo, allowing humanitarian aid to the refugees 
and opening up serious talks with the secessionists on the restoration 
of their self-rule which he abolished in 1989. But the sources, who 
spoke on condition of anonymity, said Milosevic refused to budge on 
an additional demand to deploy an international monitoring force in 
the secessionist Serbian province. The force, aimed at seeing if he 
was living up to his commitments, would include some NATO troops. 
Washington is pressing for a monitoring force because Milosevic is 
known for breaking his promises during Bosnia's 1992-95 war, as well 
for violating a pledge earlier this year to halt his offensive against 
ethnic Albanian militants in Kosovo. Holbrooke has declined comment 
on specifics of his latest meetings with Milosevic, which lasted until 
after midnight for a second straight night. He said earlier the situation 
had not changed from the previous day. There was no immediate comment
