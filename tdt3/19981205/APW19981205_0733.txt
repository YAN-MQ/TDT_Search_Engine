Nigerians took a step away from military rule towards democracy Saturday 
in local government elections seen as a test for Africa's most populous 
nation. In stark contrast to the usual frenzied commerce and paralyzing 
traffic jams of Nigerian cities, shops were closed and the government 
ordered all cars, trucks and even motorcycles off city streets and 
country highways to prevent political protests and traffic jams. Children 
played soccer in the streets while adults filed past on their way 
to the polls. Nigeria, a country filled with ethnic tension, crime 
and corruption, came to a temporary halt. Although borders were closed 
and about 100,000 riot police patrolled the major cities and rural 
villages amid fears of elections violence, the mood was generally 
buoyant. But at least one violent incident reportedly marred the elections. 
Rival supporters of two parties campaigning at a polling station near 
the town of Warri about 200 kilometers (125 miles) attacked each other 
with guns and machetes, resulting in six people dead, witnesses told 
reporters there. Police broke up the fighting, which occurred after 
the groups exchanged accusations of trying to rig the vote, the witnesses 
said. There was no official confirmation of the incident. Pickup 6th
