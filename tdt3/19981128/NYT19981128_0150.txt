Emil G. Reynolds, a globe-trotting press photographer who covered 
World War II and other events, died Nov. 22 at Monmouth Medical Center 
in Long Branch, N.J. He was 93 and lived in Long Branch. Reynolds 
was born in Vienna but grew up in Newark, N.J. He started his career 
with the old Newark Ledger before supplying his work to United Press 
International, The Associated Press and various newspapers in the 
United States and Mexico. In World War II he covered nearly all fronts, 
working in the China-Burma-India theater, the South Pacific and Europe 
and at the Yalta Conference. His fluency in German came in handy at 
the Potsdam peace conference and the war trials at Nuremberg. He photographed 
Israel's war of independence and several Arab League conferences. 
His photographs appeared on the front pages of Newsweek, Time and 
Life magazines, among others. A picture he took minutes after the 
1946 bombing of the King David Hotel in Jerusalem was published around 
the world. Reynolds is survived by a son, Paul, of Tempe, Ariz., and 
two grandsons. His wife, Clara Silverman Reynolds, died in 1991 after 
62 years of marriage. c.1998 N.Y. Times News Service Anthony Salvatore, 
a Grammy-winning recording engineer, died on Nov. 18 at Hackensack 
University Medical Center in Hackensack, N.J. He was 77 and lived 
in Hasbrouck Heights, N.J. Salvatore worked with RCA Records from 
1955 to 1993, and then retired to work independently. He won Grammys 
for recordings of ``Lohengrin'' (1966), ``Sweeney Todd'' (1979) and 
``The Heifetz Collection'' (1995), and was credited with having supervised 
more than 1,000 recordings. Among the artists he worked with were 
the conductors Fritz Reiner, Sir Thomas Beecham and Leopold Stokowski; 
the instrumentalists Artur Rubinstein, Vladimir Horowitz and Van Cliburn, 
and the vocalists Robert Merrill and Placido Domingo. He also worked 
on recordings by the Boston Symphony Orchestra, the Royal Philharmonic, 
and the New York Philharmonic. He is survived by his wife, Mary Jo; 
a daughter, Penny Salvatore Shepos of Somerset, N.J.; a son, Jack, 
of Los Angeles; two brothers, Gerald and Alfred, both of Parlin, N.J.; 
a sister, Palma Salvatore Mallory of New York, and four grandchildren.
